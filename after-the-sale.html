Guide

After the sale

When an auction sells out, a sequence of automatic steps turns the raise into a real, tradeable market. Here's exactly what happens, where the money and tokens go, and what each side ends up with.

Settlement, step by step

Settlement begins the instant the sale allocation is fully sold — it runs inside the same transaction as the final purchase. No one has to press a button.

  1. Fees are calculatedA small protocol fee is taken from two things: the funds raised, and the liquidity reserve tokens. The fee rate is the same for both.
  2. The trading pool is seededWhat's left after fees — the remaining funds and the remaining reserve tokens — is placed together into a trading pool. This pool is what makes the token tradeable.
  3. Claims openBuyers can now collect the tokens they committed to during the auction.
Fees never touch buyer tokens

The fee comes only out of the liquidity side — the raised funds and the reserve. The tokens buyers committed to are kept whole. A buyer always claims exactly what they bought.

Where everything ends up

AssetDestination
Sale allocation tokensClaimed by the buyers who committed for them
Most of the raised fundsInto the trading pool as liquidity
Most of the liquidity reserveInto the trading pool, paired with the funds
The protocol fee (funds + tokens)To the protocol's designated recipient

If settlement's automatic step fails

Settlement involves an external step — building the trading pool. If that step fails for any reason, punk.fun is designed so the sale itself does not fail. The auction simply waits in a "ready to settle" state, and the settlement can be retried by anyone. Once a retry succeeds, claims open as normal. No funds or tokens are lost in this scenario.

Claiming (buyers)

Once claims are open, each buyer claims their full entitlement in a single action. There's no deadline. See the buyer guide for the full claim walkthrough.

The liquidity position (launchers & protocol)

The trading pool created at settlement holds real value. The position representing that liquidity is controlled by the protocol, not handed to the launcher. This is a deliberate design choice: it removes the launcher's ability to pull the market's liquidity, which is a common failure mode in token launches elsewhere.

FUN launches

For a FUN launch, the pooled liquidity is placed into a staking position on the underlying exchange, with the protocol's designated recipient as the beneficiary. That recipient is also where the settlement fees and any small leftover amounts go. Over time, that staked position can earn rewards — collecting and managing those rewards is an ongoing protocol-side operation, not something the launcher or buyers need to do.

PUNK launches Coming soon

PUNK launches will run on the same engine, with post-sale assets routed to a protocol-controlled destination. This launch style isn't available yet — every launch on punk.fun today is a FUN launch, so the FUN behaviour above is what currently applies.

In short

Buyers get their tokens. The market gets its liquidity. The protocol holds the liquidity position so it can't be rugged. Everyone's outcome is fixed by the rules, not by anyone's discretion after the fact.

What "done" looks like

After a successful settlement:

  • The token has a live trading pool — anyone can buy or sell it.
  • Auction buyers can claim, then trade their claimed tokens freely.
  • The launcher's job is complete; there's nothing further they must do.
  • The launch is, in every meaningful sense, finished.
