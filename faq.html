Frequently asked questions

Short answers to the most common questions. For the full picture, follow the links into the guides.

General

What is punk.fun in one sentence?

A token launchpad that sells a token through a fair falling-price auction and automatically turns the proceeds into a live trading market.

Do I need to understand smart contracts to use it?

No. This documentation is written for non-technical users. You interact through an interface; the contract logic runs underneath without you needing to read it.

What's the difference between FUN and PUNK?

FUN creates a brand-new token for you; PUNK lets you launch a token you already have. The auction itself is identical. FUN is the style available today — PUNK is on the roadmap but not yet live. See FUN vs PUNK.

For buyers

Why didn't I get my tokens immediately after buying?

That's expected. Buying commits funds and locks in an entitlement. You claim the actual tokens after the whole sale finishes and settles. It works the same way for every buyer.

Why does the price keep changing?

It's a Dutch auction. Within a tranche the price falls steadily; when a tranche sells out the next one starts higher. You buy when the falling price reaches a level you're comfortable with.

Can I overpay if the price jumps while I'm buying?

You can protect yourself by setting a maximum price on your buy. If the price has risen above it, the buy is rejected rather than filled at a worse price. Setting this is recommended.

What if I try to buy more than a tranche has left?

You receive what's left of that tranche, and the excess funds are automatically refunded to you. You won't be silently pulled into the next tranche.

Is there a deadline to claim my tokens?

No. Once claims are open after settlement, you can claim at any time.

What happens if the auction never sells out?

In this version, selling out is the only way an auction completes — there's no time-based expiry and no built-in refund path for committed funds. If a sale stalls, committed funds can remain committed indefinitely. Only buy into launches you believe will complete.

For launchers

How much of my token gets sold?

Half. The launch supply is split 50/50: half is the sale allocation sold in the auction, half is the liquidity reserve used to seed the trading pool. This split is fixed.

Do I set the fees and the liquidity destination?

No. Fees, the liquidity destination, and the 50/50 split are set by the protocol, not the launcher. You control the auction parameters — price, tranche sizes, decay, and start.

I sent the wrong amount of tokens. Can I fix it?

Yes — but only before you confirm the inventory configuration. Up to that point you can withdraw the tokens back out. After configuration, withdrawal is closed. Always check the displayed balance first.

Do I control the liquidity after the sale?

No. The liquidity position is held by the protocol, not the launcher. This is intentional — it prevents the launcher from removing the market's liquidity later.

What do I need to do during the auction?

Nothing. Once it's funded and configured, the auction runs on its own and settlement is automatic. Your real job is building demand before the launch.

Fees, risk & safety

What fees are there?

A small protocol fee is taken at settlement from the funds raised and from the liquidity reserve. It is never taken from the tokens buyers committed to — buyers always claim their full amount. (You'll also pay normal network transaction fees, as with any on-chain action.)

Can the token supply be inflated after launch?

For FUN tokens, no — they're fixed-supply with no minting function and no owner. (PUNK launches, which let you bring an existing token, are on the roadmap but not yet available; when they arrive, that will depend on the token you bring.)

Is buying a newly launched token safe?

No token launch is "safe." Newly launched tokens are highly volatile and can lose value quickly. punk.fun makes the mechanics fair and transparent, but it cannot make a token a good investment. This documentation is not financial advice — only commit funds you can afford to lose.
